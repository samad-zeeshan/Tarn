Los Alamos National Laboratory recorded every login on its network for 58 days. That comes to - events. During those same 58 days a red team ran a real attack, and the lab published the exact list of which logins were the attack.

So there is an answer key. That is what makes this worth building. Every part of the project can ask a better question than how many rows are there. It can ask: would this have caught them?

02 · making it faster measured on a laptop

Getting it to run faster, without cheating

The job that summarises a billion logins was slow. The fix was to stop reading the same data twice. It used to go through every event once to count what each person did, then go through them all over again to work out which machines they had never used before. Now it builds one shared summary and both steps read that.

The easy way to fake a speed-up is to quietly do less work. So all four versions were run and their answers were compared against each other. They match exactly.

Proof it is not just doing less work
A faster wrong answer is not an improvement

Every version produced the same number of rows, and the same fingerprint of the data inside them. If any version had skipped work, that fingerprint would have changed. It did not.

One honest note on the second row. Spark would normally pick that trick by itself, so that row shows what the slower approach costs, rather than a bug that got fixed. Saying so is the difference between a measurement and a boast.

03 · as it happens replayed on a laptop

Counting logins as they arrive

Same data, but pushed through a live pipe instead of read from a file. Events go into a message queue, a streaming job picks them up, and every minute of activity gets counted per person. Events that turn up late still land in the right minute, and if the job is killed and restarted it carries on from where it stopped without counting anything twice.

The delay below is mostly the job waiting to see whether any stragglers turn up before it closes a minute off. That waiting is the point. If the number were near zero it would mean the job was not waiting at all, and late events were being thrown away.

What the run actually did
The settings and the throughput, exactly as they were configured

This is a replay from a file into a message queue on the same laptop as the thing reading it. No network, no cluster, nothing else competing for the machine. It measures this pipeline, not a real production system.

04 · where an attacker can go real results, replayed

Two people who use the same machine are connected

That is the whole idea. If someone steals my account, and I log into a server that a colleague also logs into, they now have a route towards that colleague. Chain those steps together and you have a path through the company.

Pick any two people below and the shortest route between them lights up. These routes are real answers from a graph database, worked out in advance, because your browser cannot run that kind of query. Turn on the overlay to see the route the attacker actually took.

Pick two people to trace a route.
The machines everybody uses
This is the finding that held up. A few machines are used by tens of thousands of people each. That is what makes the network so small. Almost everyone is two steps from almost everyone else, through one of these. Lock these few down and you break most of the routes. That is something you can act on. "The attacker can reach nearly every machine" is not.

05 · the part that failed

Logging in at odd hours had to be measured, not guessed

The data does not come with clock times. It only says how many seconds had passed since the recording began. So "this person logged in at 3am" is not something you can read off it.

The first attempt to find the quiet hours found nothing. The line was almost flat. That turned out to be correct. Most logins are not people at all, they are machines checking in with each other around the clock, and they were drowning out the humans. Separate them out and the human rhythm is obvious. A dip overnight, a peak mid-morning.

And then it caught nothing. This is the most careful piece of work in the project, and as a way of spotting the attacker it scored zero out of 181. Being careful does not entitle you to a result. It is in the table above, at the bottom, with a zero next to it.

06 · find more like this one running in your browser

Describe a day as 52 numbers, then look for days that rhyme

Every person-day is turned into a list of 52 numbers describing how that person behaved: how much they did, how often they failed, what hours they were active, and what kinds of login they used. Similar behaviour ends up with similar numbers. Then you can ask the computer for the nearest matches, the same way an image search finds similar photos.

Nothing from the answer key goes into those numbers. If it did, the search would just be finding the answers, and every result below would be worthless.

The result, plainly
One of these is a good idea and the other is not

As a way of spotting the attack on its own, this lost. Told to go and find unusual behaviour with no other help, it does worse than the simple rules at every budget but one, and the one it wins is the rule that caught nothing at all. Being unusual and being an attacker are not the same thing. Most unusual days are just somebody having an unusual day.

As a way of finding more of an attack you already found, it is very good. Hand it a single confirmed bad day and ask for lookalikes, and - of what it hands back is also part of the attack. That is - better than picking days at random. It cannot find the first one for you. It can find the rest.

Where the data came from

Los Alamos National Laboratory, 2015. Real logins from a real network with the names taken out, so a person is just U292 and a machine is just C1065. Free for anyone to use. The list of attack logins is published alongside it.

What this is not

It is not a production system. It runs on one laptop. It has never handled a billion events a day. It handled a billion events once. Every number on this page comes from a file in the project, so if something was not measured, it is not here.
